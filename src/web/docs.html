Core Concepts

A Yield Curve represents the relationship between interest rates and different maturities (tenors) for a given currency and credit quality. In quantitative finance, a yield curve is never represented simply as a set of par yields. Instead, it is resolved into a continuous function of Discount Factors or Zero-Coupon Yields.

Discount Factors (DF)

The discount factor D(0, T) is the present value of $1 to be received at time T in the future. It represents the ultimate building block of asset pricing. Discount factors are monotonically decreasing with respect to maturity time:

D(0, T) = e-z(T) × T
Relationship between Discount Factor D(0,T) and Zero-Coupon Rate z(T)

Zero Rates

The Zero-Coupon Rate z(T) is the annualized interest rate earned on a single cash flow maturing at time T, assuming continuous compounding. If we bootstrap the discount factors, we can extract the continuous zero rate at any point via:

z(T) = -
ln( D(0, T) ) T

Market Instruments

Interest rate curves are built using various highly liquid, standard market instruments. Because different tenors have different liquid vehicles, we use different instrument types across the maturity spectrum.

1. Short-Term: Cash Deposits / O/N Rates

For maturities from overnight (O/N) up to 3 months, we use simple cash deposit rates. The discount factor is computed directly using the day-count fraction τ (e.g. ACT/360):

D(0, T) =
1 1 + Rate × τ

2. Medium-Term: Interest Rate Futures

For tenors between 3 months and 2 years, quarterly interest rate futures (like SOFR Futures) are the most liquid instruments. A future is quoted as a price index P. The implied forward interest rate is:

Rate = 100 - P

Discount factors are linked sequentially across future maturity dates:

D(0, Tend) =
D(0, Tstart) 1 + Rate × τ

3. Long-Term: Interest Rate Swaps (IRS)

Beyond 2 years, par Swaps are the standard. A swap is an agreement to exchange a fixed coupon rate for a floating rate (e.g., SOFR). At inception, the swap rate is set so that the Net Present Value (NPV) of the swap is exactly zero.

Curve Bootstrapping

Bootstrapping is the process of sequentially solving for discount factors starting from today (T=0) and moving outward in time. Because long-term swaps pay periodic coupons that depend on intermediate discount factors, we must solve them in chronological order.

OIS Curve Bootstrapping

For OIS (Overnight Indexed Swap) curves, we use cash rates up to the futures cutoff, then transition to futures contracts, and finally swap par rates.

US Treasury Curve Bootstrapping

For US Government bonds, the curve builder uses a different dual approach:

  1. Treasury Bills (T-Bills): These are zero-coupon instruments. The discount factor is computed directly from the price:
    D(0, T) =
    Price 100
  2. Treasury Notes & Bonds: These pay semi-annual coupons. We bootstrap the final discount factor at the bond's maturity TN by subtracting the present value of all intermediate coupon payments from the bond price:
    D(0, TN) =
    Price -
    C2
    ∑i=1N-1 D(0, ti)     1 +
    C2
    Treasury Bootstrap Formula (Price is divided by 100, C is coupon rate decimal)
    Since intermediate coupon payment dates ti do not always align with known bond maturities, we use linear zero-rate interpolation to find those intermediate discount factors D(0, ti).

Liquidity Selection

In real markets, different instruments can mature at the exact same time. For example, a 2-Year Swap and a 2-Year Future contract will expire within days of each other. Putting both into a bootstrapping engine causes numerical instability and "noise" in the curve.

The 0.05-Year (18 Days) Overlap Rule

The Liquidity Auto-Selection Engine scans the list of market inputs. If any two instruments mature within 0.05 years (~18 days) of each other, they are flagged as overlapping.

Spread-Based Selection

To choose the best node automatically, the engine evaluates the user-supplied Spread (bps) (bid-ask spread, which is the direct proxy for liquidity).

  • The instrument with the narrowest spread (most liquid) is flagged as Active and used to build the curve.
  • The other instrument is marked as Skipped and excluded from the bootstrap grid.

Pricing Skipped Instruments

Even though skipped instruments do not construct the curve, the engine still prices them using the finished curve. This allows traders to immediately see the valuation discrepancy between a highly liquid instrument and an illiquid overlapping one.

Cubic Splines

A bootstrapped curve only yields interest rates at the specific maturity dates (knots) of the input instruments. To price a swap that matures at an intermediate date, we must interpolate.

Why Cubic Splines?

Linear interpolation creates "kinks" in the yield curve, meaning the first derivative is discontinuous. This causes severe spikes in the forward rates. A Natural Cubic Spline fits piecewise cubic polynomials between each knot:

Si(t) = ai + bi(t - ti) + ci(t - ti)2 + di(t - ti)3

It guarantees that the zero rate curve is completely smooth, meaning:

  • The zero curve is continuous: Si(ti+1) = Si+1(ti+1)
  • The slope (first derivative) is continuous: S'i(ti+1) = S'i+1(ti+1)
  • The curvature (second derivative) is continuous: S''i(ti+1) = S''i+1(ti+1)

Swap Valuation & Risk

Once the curve is built, we can value any interest rate swap portfolio.

Swap Net Present Value (NPV)

The NPV of a swap is the difference between the Present Value (PV) of its floating leg and the PV of its fixed leg:

NPVPayer = PV(Floating Leg) - PV(Fixed Leg)

Leg Calculations

  • Fixed Leg PV:
    PV(Fixed) = Notional × Rfixed × ∑ τi × D(0, ti)
  • Floating Leg PV: For a standard OIS swap, the floating leg present value simplifies to the difference in discount factors between start and end (due to no-arbitrage):
    PV(Floating) = Notional × [ D(0, Tstart) - D(0, Tend) ]

Swap Delta / PVBP (Price Value of a Basis Point)

The PVBP (also known as DV01) measures the portfolio's interest rate sensitivity. It is the dollar change in NPV if the entire yield curve shifts parallelly by 1 basis point (0.01% or 0.0001).

The pricer accomplishes this by:

  1. Shifting all zero rates up by 1 basis point: zbumped(t) = z(t) + 0.0001
  2. Recalculating the bumped discount factors: Dbumped(t) = e-zbumped(t) × t
  3. Re-valuing the swap portfolio to get NPVbumped.
  4. PVBP is the net change: PVBP = | NPVbumped - NPVbase |
