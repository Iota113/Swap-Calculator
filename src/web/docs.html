1. Core Concepts & App Guide

A Yield Curve represents the graphical and mathematical relationship between interest rates and different maturities (tenors) for a given currency and credit quality. In quantitative finance, a yield curve is represented as a set of continuous Discount Factors or Zero-Coupon Yields.

Our web application allows users to build and visualize these curves in real-time, pricing customized interest rate swaps against the resulting curve.

Discount Factors (DF)

The discount factor D(0, T) is the present value of \$1 to be received at maturity R in the future. It represents the ultimate building block of asset pricing. Discount factors are monotonically decreasing with respect to maturity time:

$$D(0, T) = e^{-z(T) \cdot T}$$
Relationship between Discount Factor $D(0,T)$ and continuous Zero Rate $z(T)$

Zero Rates

The Zero-Coupon Rate z(T) is the annualized interest rate earned on a single cash flow maturing at time T, assuming continuous compounding. If we bootstrap the discount factors, we extract the continuous zero rate at any point via:

$$z(T) = -\frac{\ln(D(0, T))}{T}$$

2. Market Instruments

Interest rate curves are built using various highly liquid, standard market instruments. Because different tenors have different liquid vehicles, we use different instrument types across the maturity spectrum:

1. Short-Term: Cash Deposits / O/N Rates

For maturities from overnight (O/N) up to 3 months, simple money market cash deposits are used. The discount factor is computed directly from the rate quote using the day count fraction $\tau$ (usually ACT/360):

$$D(0, T) = \frac{1}{1 + \text{Rate} \cdot \tau}$$

2. Medium-Term: Interest Rate Futures

For tenors between 3 months and 2 years, quarterly interest rate futures (such as SOFR Futures) are the most liquid instruments. A future contract is quoted as a price index P. The implied forward interest rate is:

$$\text{Rate} = 100 - P$$

Discount factors are linked sequentially across future contract expiry dates:

$$D(0, T_{\text{end}}) = \frac{D(0, T_{\text{start}})}{1 + \text{Rate} \cdot \tau}$$

3. Long-Term: Interest Rate Swaps (IRS)

Beyond 2 years, par Swaps are the standard. A swap is an agreement to exchange a fixed coupon rate for a floating rate index (e.g. SOFR). At inception, the fixed swap rate is set so that the Net Present Value (NPV) of the swap is exactly zero.

3. Time Value & Conventions

The present value (PV) of a future cash flow C received at maturity point n is dictated by the discount factor F_n (represented as D(0, t_n) in curve builders). In discrete compounding terms, the relationship is:

$$PV = \frac{C}{(1 + R)^n} = C \cdot F_n$$
Discount factor representation: $F_n = \frac{1}{(1 + R)^n}$

Day Count Fractions

Let $\alpha_{i-1, i}$ denote the day count fraction between two dates D_{i-1} and D_i. Based on standard market conventions, these fractions are calculated as:

  • Actual/365 (Fixed / Bond Basis): $\alpha = \frac{D_i - D_{i-1}}{365}$
  • Actual/360 (Money Market Basis): $\alpha = \frac{D_i - D_{i-1}}{360}$
  • 30/360 Basis: Assumes 30 days per month and 360 days per year.

Compounding Frequency Conversion

When converting an interest rate r_1 with compounding frequency n_1 to an equivalent rate r_2 with frequency n_2, we enforce compound balance:

$$\left(1 + \frac{r_1}{n_1}\right)^{n_1} = \left(1 + \frac{r_2}{n_2}\right)^{n_2} \implies r_2 = n_2 \left[ \left(1 + \frac{r_1}{n_1}\right)^{\frac{n_1}{n_2}} - 1 \right]$$

Annuity Present Value

For an equal payment annuity stream yielding regular cash flows C over n periods at rate i, the present value aggregates to:

$$PV = C \cdot \left[ \frac{1 - (1+i)^{-n}}{i} \right]$$

4. Curve Bootstrapping

Bootstrapping is the process of sequentially solving for discount factors starting from today (T=0, F_0 = 1) out into the future.

OIS & Swap Curve Bootstrapping

For overnight indexed swap (OIS) curves, we use cash deposits for the ultra-short-term, interest rate futures to span the medium-term (up to the futures cutoff point), and par interest rate swaps for the long-term.

Consider an n-year interest rate swap with a fixed par swap rate R_n and principal P. Setting the PV of the fixed coupon payments equal to the PV of the floating payments yields the bootstrapped discount factor formula at grid node n:

$$F_n = \frac{1 - R_n \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} \alpha_{i-1,i} F_i}{1 + R_n \alpha_{n-1,n}}$$
Sequential Swap Bootstrapping Formula

Extracting Zero Rates

From the discount factors F(0, T), we calculate the continuously compounded zero rate r(T) via:

$$F(0, T) = e^{-r(T) \cdot T} \implies r(T) = -\frac{\ln(F(0, T))}{T}$$

Par Yield Re-expression

Conversely, if we possess the discount curve, the par swap rate R_n for a tenor maturing at step n can be backed out as:

$$R_n = \frac{1 - F_n}{\sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_{i-1,i} F_i}$$

Short-Term Money Market Instruments

For single-period cash deposits or bills maturing at time m:

$$F_m = \frac{1}{1 + R_m \alpha_{0,m}}$$
Where $R_m$ is the deposit rate quote.

Similarly, we extract the implied forward rate (e.g. R_6M) via:

$$R_{\text{6M}} = \frac{1}{\alpha_{0,\text{6M}}} \left( \frac{1}{F_{\text{6M}}} - 1 \right)$$

US Treasury Curve Bootstrapping

For US Government bonds, the curve builder uses a different dual approach:

  1. Treasury Bills (T-Bills): These are zero-coupon instruments. The discount factor is computed directly from the price:
    $$F_m = \frac{\text{Price}}{100}$$
  2. Treasury Notes & Bonds: These pay semi-annual coupons. We bootstrap the final discount factor at the bond's maturity T_N by subtracting the present value of all intermediate coupon payments from the bond price:
    $$F_n = \frac{\text{Price} - \frac{C}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} \alpha_{i-1,i} F_i}{1 + \frac{C}{2}}$$
    Treasury Bootstrap Formula (Price is divided by 100, C is coupon rate decimal)
    Since intermediate coupon payment dates t_i do not always align with known bond maturities, we use linear zero-rate interpolation to find those intermediate discount factors F_i.

5. Yield Curve Interpolation

Because bootstrapping only gives interest rates at the specific maturity dates (knots) of the input instruments, intermediate dates require interpolation.

Continuous / Exponential Rate Interpolation

To find a discount factor F_a at date D_a between two known grid nodes D_1 and D_2, assuming a continuously compounded rate R_a:

$$F_a = e^{-\mathcal{R}_a(D_a - D_0)}$$
Interpolated rate $\mathcal{R}_a = (1-\lambda)\mathcal{R}_1 + \lambda\mathcal{R}_2$ where $\lambda = \frac{D_a - D_1}{D_2 - D_1}$

This translates geometrically to:

$$F_a = F_1^{(1-\lambda)\frac{D_a-D_0}{D_1-D_0}} \cdot F_2^{\lambda\frac{D_a-D_0}{D_2-D_0}}$$

Natural Cubic Splines

To avoid sudden shifts in forward rates, our engine uses a **Cubic Spline** on zero rates. This fits a third-degree polynomial between each knot:

$$S_i(t) = a_i + b_i(t - t_i) + c_i(t - t_i)^2 + d_i(t - t_i)^3$$

This mathematical framework enforces continuity of the curve, its first derivative (slope), and its second derivative (curvature) across all knots, providing a smooth zero rate yield curve.

  • Zero Rate Continuity: S_i(t_{i+1}) = S_{i+1}(t_{i+1})
  • Slope Continuity: S'_i(t_{i+1}) = S'_{i+1}(t_{i+1})
  • Curvature Continuity: S''_i(t_{i+1}) = S''_{i+1}(t_{i+1})

6. Swap Leg Valuation Frameworks

The Fixed Leg

The present value (PV) of receiving or paying a fixed rate R_fixed is the sum of discounted fixed coupon flows:

$$PV_{\text{fixed}} = \text{Notional} \cdot R_{\text{fixed}} \cdot \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_{i-1,i} \cdot D(0, t_i)$$

The Standard Floating Leg

The implied forward index rate $\Phi_{a,b}$ spanning from date D_a to D_b is:

$$\Phi_{a,b} = \frac{1}{\alpha_{a,b}} \left( \frac{F_a}{F_b} - 1 \right)$$

Valuing a single standard floating cash flow payment at payment point F_b yields:

$$PV_{\text{floating}} = \text{Notional} \cdot \Phi_{a,b} \cdot \alpha_{a,b} \cdot F_b = \text{Notional} \cdot [F_a - F_b]$$

For a multi-period standard floating leg, the intermediate discount factors collapse telescopically:

$$PV_{\text{total\_floating}} = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Notional} \cdot (F_{i-1} - F_i) = \text{Notional} \cdot [F_{\text{start}} - F_{\text{end}}]$$
No-arbitrage PV of standard floating leg

Interest Rate Futures Chain Bootstrapping

When constructing a discount curve from a serial chain of quarterly interest rate futures prices P_j, the subsequent boundary discount factor F_{iF} is calculated recursively:

$$F_{iF} = F_{1F} \prod_{j=1}^{i-1} \frac{1}{1 + (100 - P_j)\alpha_{jF,(j+1)F}}$$

Mismatched / Basis Swap Floating Leg

When the rate reset index period ( $\alpha_{b,c}$ differs from the accrual payment period fraction $\alpha_{a,b}$ ), the cancellation breaks down, requiring explicit valuation:

$$PV_{\text{mismatched}} = P \left( \frac{F_b}{F_c} - 1 \right) \frac{\alpha_{a,b}}{\alpha_{b,c}} F_b$$

Floating Leg with Amortizing Principal

If the swap principal P_i changes over time, the telescoping sum expands explicitly into:

$$PV_{\text{amortizing}} = F_1 P_1 + \sum_{i=2}^{n-1} F_i (P_i - P_{i-1}) - F_n P_{n-1}$$

Forward Swap Rate

The forward swap rate R is the rate that sets the present value of a newly initialized forward swap to 0:

$$R = \frac{F_{\text{start}} - F_{\text{end}}}{\sum_i \alpha_{i-1,i} F_i}$$

7. Risk Management: Delta Vectors & Hedging

Hedge ratios and interest rate exposures are calculated using partial derivative sensitivities of portfolio present value with respect to the input quotes R':

$$\text{Delta}(\mathcal{P}) = \frac{1}{100} \times \frac{\partial PV(\mathcal{P})}{\partial \mathbf{F}} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{F}}{\partial \mathbf{R}'}$$

Consider an isolated off-grid cash flow C occurring at date D_a, which sits between key grid maturities D_1 and D_2. Its valuation is sensitive to movements at both boundaries:

$$PV(C) = F_a \cdot C \implies \frac{\partial PV(C)}{\partial F_i} = \frac{\partial F_a}{\partial F_i} \cdot C$$

The Mathematical Bias of Non-Linear Interpolation

While a linear interpolation methodology guarantees matching present values, an exponential curve construction method generates an exact hedging friction. Due to non-linear tracking errors:

$$\frac{\partial F_a}{\partial F_1} F_1 + \frac{\partial F_a}{\partial F_2} F_2 \neq F_a \implies PV(\text{Pair}) \neq PV(C)$$
Consequently, maintaining an identical delta risk posture over an exponentially interpolated curve introduces a subtle accounting value mismatch.

PVBP / DV01 Risk Formulation

The Present Value of a Basis Point (PVBP), synonymous with DV01 (Dollar Value of an 01), records the absolute variation in NPV caused by a parallel shift across the zero rate curve:

$$PVBP = \left| \frac{PV(R + 1\,\text{bp}) - PV(R - 1\,\text{bp})}{2} \right| \approx \frac{\partial PV}{\partial R} \times 0.0001$$

Pricer Implementation (One-Sided Parallel Shift)

In our quantitative calculation engine, the Swap Pricer computes the PVBP (DV01) using a one-sided +1 basis point shift:

  1. Base NPV: Value the portfolio swaps using the constructed baseline zero rate curve to get $NPV_{\text{base}}$.
  2. Zero Rate Shift: Apply a +1 bp (+0.0001) parallel shift to all continuous zero rates: $z_{\text{bumped}}(t) = z(t) + 0.0001$
  3. Re-calculate Discount Factors: Convert the bumped zero rates back to discount factors: $D_{\text{bumped}}(t) = e^{-z_{\text{bumped}}(t) \cdot t}$
  4. Re-value Swaps: Re-price the swap portfolio with the bumped discount factors to get $NPV_{\text{bumped}}$.
  5. Calculate Change: The Swap Delta (PVBP) is the absolute net change: $PVBP = |NPV_{\text{bumped}} - NPV_{\text{base}}|$

For a standard fixed swap leg, the PVBP aggregates directly into a measure of the underlying annuity string metric:

$$PVBP_{\text{fixed}} = \text{Notional} \cdot \alpha \cdot \sum_{i=1}^n F_i \times 0.0001$$

Optimal Hedge Ratios

To neutralize the directional interest rate exposure of a swap portfolio using a liquid benchmark hedging contract, we calculate the hedge weight W_hedge:

$$W_{\text{hedge}} = - \frac{PVBP_{\text{portfolio}}}{PVBP_{\text{hedging\_instrument}}}$$
Ensures that $PVBP_{\text{portfolio}} + W_{\text{hedge}} \times PVBP_{\text{hedging\_instrument}} = 0$

8. The Liquidity Auto-Selection Engine

When building yield curves, including instruments with duplicate or overlapping maturities (e.g. a 2Y Swap and a 2Y Future contract maturing within days of each other) leads to numerical instability, resulting in localized zero-rate spikes.

The 0.05-Year Overlap Rule

The engine scans the maturity times of all active instruments. Overlaps are detected using a 0.05-year window (~18 days): $|t_{\text{instrument}_1} - t_{\text{instrument}_2}| < 0.05 \text{ years}$

Spread-Based Comparison & Selection

$$\text{Spread (in bps)} = \frac{\text{Ask Price} - \text{Bid Price}}{\text{Mid Price}} \times 10,000$$

For any overlapping group, the engine compares the user-specified **Spread (bps)** column, which represents the **bid-ask spread** (serving as a proxy for transaction costs and liquidity, and not the yield spread):

$$\text{Instrument}_{\text{selected}} = \arg\min_{\text{instruments}} (\text{Spread}_{\text{bps}})$$
  • The instrument with the minimum spread is flagged as Active.
  • All other overlapping instruments are marked as Skipped (Illiquid Overlap) to protect the curve building matrix.
  • Theoretical Valuation Comparison: The engine still prices the skipped instruments against the generated curve, showing the valuation gap between actual market quotes and clean curve predictions. This allows traders to evaluate discrepancies.
